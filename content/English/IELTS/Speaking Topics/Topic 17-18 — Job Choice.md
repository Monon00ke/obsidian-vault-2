## Sample Answer — Part 1

> **Examiner:** What do you think is the most important factor when choosing a job?

> **In my opinion**, the most important factor is **job satisfaction**. **To put it another way**, you should enjoy what you do because you spend most of your time at work. **However**, salary is also important — you need to make a living.

> **Generally speaking**, I think a good **work-life balance** is crucial. **More often than not**, people who work 60 hours a week burn out quickly, no matter how much they earn.

## Sample Answer — Part 2 (Describe your ideal job)

> **The first thing that comes to mind** is a job where I can combine my skills in programming with my interest in technology. **What strikes me the most** is the opportunity to work on meaningful projects.

> **In my ideal job**, I would work in a team of talented developers, with a modern **tech stack** and regular **code reviews**. The company would offer **flexible hours** and the option to **work remotely** sometimes. **Moreover**, I would appreciate a good **learning budget** for courses and conferences.

> **Of course**, the salary should be competitive — enough to live comfortably and save for the future. But **at the end of the day**, I believe that enjoying your work and growing professionally is more important than a huge paycheck.

> **That's my take on it.**

## Sample Answer — Part 3

> **Examiner:** Do you think people today change jobs more often than in the past?

> **Without a doubt**, yes — people change jobs much more frequently now. **In the past**, it was common to work for one company for 30 years and get a gold watch at retirement. **Today**, the average person changes jobs every 3–5 years.

> **In my opinion**, there are several reasons for this. **First of all**, the **job market** is much more dynamic now — there are more opportunities. **Moreover**, people value **career growth** and won't stay in a dead-end job. **Finally**, **remote work** has opened up global opportunities — you're not limited to companies in your city.

> **However**, there are downsides too. Frequent job changes can mean less **job security** and less loyalty from employers. **That being said**, I think it's a positive trend because it forces companies to treat employees better to retain them.

## IELTS Writing Examples

### Essay 1 — Factors for choosing a job

**Prompt:** What factors should people consider when choosing a job? Is salary the most important factor?

> Choosing a job is one of the most important decisions a person makes. While salary is certainly a key factor, I believe that other considerations are equally, if not more, important for long-term satisfaction.

> **Undoubtedly**, salary matters. People need to earn enough to support themselves and their families. **To give you an example**, a person with a high salary but no free time may be financially comfortable but unhappy. **However**, once a certain income level is reached, additional money does not necessarily increase happiness.

> **In my opinion**, **job satisfaction** and **work-life balance** are more important in the long run. Working in a positive environment with supportive colleagues and interesting tasks makes a huge difference to mental health. **Moreover**, opportunities for **career growth** and **professional development** are essential — a dead-end job with no prospects can be demoralising, regardless of the pay.

> **Furthermore**, factors like location, commute time, and flexible working hours significantly affect quality of life. **Remote work**, for example, has become increasingly valued because it saves time and reduces stress.

> **In conclusion**, while salary is important, it should not be the sole factor. **At the end of the day**, a good balance of income, satisfaction, and personal time leads to the happiest career.

### Essay 2 — Job hopping: good or bad?

**Prompt:** In the past, people often stayed in one job for their entire career. Today, changing jobs frequently has become common. Is this a positive or negative trend?

> The nature of work has changed dramatically over the past few decades. While some view frequent job changes negatively, I believe it is largely a positive development.

> **In the past**, job stability was highly valued. People worked for one company for 30 or 40 years, earned a pension, and received a gold watch at retirement. **Loyalty** was rewarded, and changing jobs was seen as disloyal or unreliable.

> **Today**, the situation is different. **The job market** is more dynamic, and people change jobs every 3–5 years on average. **In my opinion**, this is beneficial for several reasons. **First of all**, it allows people to **develop a wider range of skills** by working in different environments. **Secondly**, frequent changes often lead to **salary increases** — staying in one place often means slower financial growth. **Finally**, if you are unhappy at work, leaving is better than staying and burning out.

> **However**, there are downsides. Too many short-term jobs can look bad on a CV, and some employers value long-term commitment. **Furthermore**, constant change can be stressful.

> **In conclusion**, changing jobs several times during your career is normal and often beneficial. **At the end of the day**, what matters most is that you are growing, learning, and satisfied with your work.

### Letter 1 — Formal: Job application

> Dear Mr Smith,

> I am writing to apply for the position of Junior Software Developer at your company, which I saw advertised on LinkedIn.

> **I recently graduated** from Smolensk State University with a degree in Applied Mathematics and Computer Science. During my studies, I gained experience in Python, Java, and web development. **I also completed** an internship at a local IT company, where I worked on developing web applications.

> **I am particularly interested** in your company because of its focus on innovative technology and professional development. **I believe** my technical skills and enthusiasm make me a suitable candidate for this role.

> **I have attached** my CV and a cover letter. **I would welcome** the opportunity to discuss my application further at an interview.

> **I look forward to** hearing from you.

> Yours sincerely,
> [Your Name]

### Letter 2 — Informal: Giving career advice to a friend

> Dear Max,

> Thanks for your letter! I was sorry to hear you're unhappy at work — that's really tough.

> **To be honest**, I think you should consider looking for a new job. If you're not growing professionally and the **work-life balance** is bad, staying there won't help anyone. **In my opinion**, the most important thing is to feel motivated and appreciated.

> **Why not** update your CV and start looking? There are plenty of IT companies offering **remote work** with good salaries and **flexible hours**. **To give you an example**, my friend switched jobs last year and doubled his salary while working from home three days a week.

> **At the end of the day**, life is too short to stay in a job you hate. Let me know if you need help with your CV!

> Best,
> Stepan
