## Types of Energy

### Non-Renewable (Невозобновляемые)

| Source | How it Works | Issues |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| **Coal** | Burned to produce electricity | Very polluting, high CO2 |
| **Oil** | Refined into fuel for transport | Limited supply, pollution |
| **Natural gas** | Burned for heating, electricity | Cleaner than coal, but still emits CO2 |
| **Nuclear** | Splitting atoms (fission) | Low emissions, but radioactive waste |

### Renewable (Возобновляемые)

| Source | How it Works | Advantages |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Solar** | Solar panels convert sunlight | Abundant, getting cheaper |
| **Wind** | Turbines turned by wind | Clean, efficient in windy areas |
| **Hydroelectric** | Water flow turns turbines | Reliable, established technology |
| **Geothermal** | Heat from inside the Earth | Constant, regardless of weather |
| **Biomass** | Burning organic matter | Uses waste, carbon-neutral |

## Key Points

### Почему нужно заменять невозобновляемые

| Problem | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Finite resources** | Coal, oil, gas will run out eventually |
| **Pollution** | Burning fossil fuels emits CO2 and harmful particles |
| **Climate change** | Fossil fuels are the main cause of global warming |
| **Price volatility** | Oil prices fluctuate due to geopolitics |
| **Energy security** | Dependence on oil-exporting countries |

### Как происходит энергетический переход

| Stage | What Happens |
|-------|-------------|
| **Current** | Still heavily dependent on fossil fuels |
| **In progress** | Solar and wind are growing rapidly |
| **Future** | Goal: carbon-neutral by 2050 |
| **Challenges** | Storage (batteries), grid infrastructure, cost |

### Pros and Cons of Each Renewable

| Source | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|
| **Solar** | Abundant, quiet, cheap | Doesn't work at night, needs space |
| **Wind** | Clean, efficient | Noisy, kills birds, needs wind |
| **Hydro** | Reliable, cheap | Dams destroy ecosystems |
| **Nuclear** | Low emissions, powerful | Radioactive waste, safety risks |

## Sample Answer — Part 1

> **Examiner:** Do you think about how much energy you use?

> **To be honest**, I try to be conscious of my energy consumption, but I could do better. **Generally speaking**, I turn off lights when I leave a room and unplug devices when not in use.

> **In my opinion**, small individual actions matter, but the real change needs to happen at the government and industry level — switching to **renewable energy** sources like **solar** and **wind power**.

## Sample Answer — Part 3

> **Examiner:** Do you think renewable energy can replace fossil fuels completely?

> **That's a good question.** **In my opinion**, yes, eventually renewable energy will replace fossil fuels — but it will take time. **Generally speaking**, the technology is already there: **solar panels** and **wind turbines** are becoming cheaper and more efficient every year.

> **However**, there are significant challenges. **First of all**, renewable energy is intermittent — the sun doesn't always shine and the wind doesn't always blow. That's why we need better **battery storage** technology. **Moreover**, upgrading the **power grid** to handle distributed energy is expensive.

> **That being said**, many countries are making impressive progress. **To give you an example**, Denmark now produces more than 50% of its electricity from wind power. **At the end of the day**, I believe a mix of solar, wind, hydro, and nuclear is the way forward.

> **Without a doubt**, the transition to clean energy is essential for fighting climate change and ensuring a sustainable future.

## IELTS Writing Examples

### Essay 1 — Can renewable energy replace fossil fuels?

**Prompt:** Some people believe that renewable energy can completely replace fossil fuels in the near future. Others think this is unrealistic. Discuss both views and give your opinion.

> The transition from **fossil fuels** to **renewable energy** is one of the most important challenges of our time. While some are optimistic about a rapid shift, I believe that a complete replacement will take longer than many expect.

> **On the one hand**, the potential of renewable energy is enormous. **Solar power** and **wind power** have become significantly cheaper over the past decade, and many countries are investing heavily in them. **To give you an example**, Denmark now produces more than 50% of its electricity from wind turbines. **Furthermore**, advances in **battery technology** are solving the problem of intermittency — we can now store energy for use when the sun is not shining or the wind is not blowing.

> **On the other hand**, there are significant obstacles. **First of all**, renewable energy is still intermittent — solar panels do not work at night and wind turbines need wind. **Grid infrastructure** needs a massive upgrade to handle distributed energy sources. **Moreover**, some industries, like aviation and heavy manufacturing, are difficult to decarbonise with current technology. **Nuclear power**, while low-carbon, raises concerns about radioactive waste.

> **In my opinion**, a complete replacement will take several decades. We will need a diverse **energy mix** that includes solar, wind, hydro, nuclear, and emerging technologies like **hydrogen fuel cells**.

> **In conclusion**, while renewable energy will certainly play a dominant role in the future, a complete replacement of fossil fuels will require time, investment, and technological breakthroughs.

### Essay 2 — Nuclear power: solution or problem?

**Prompt:** Nuclear power is a controversial energy source. Do the benefits outweigh the risks?

> Nuclear power is one of the most debated energy sources. While it offers significant advantages in terms of low carbon emissions, I believe the risks are too high to consider it a primary solution.

> **On the one hand**, nuclear power is a reliable source of **baseload power** that produces virtually no CO2 during operation. **Unlike solar or wind**, it is not affected by weather conditions. **A single nuclear plant** can power a whole city for decades. **Moreover**, modern reactor designs are safer than ever before.

> **On the other hand**, the risks are substantial. **Nuclear accidents**, though rare, can be catastrophic — Chernobyl and Fukushima are grim reminders. **Furthermore**, the problem of **radioactive waste** remains unsolved; it stays dangerous for thousands of years. **Finally**, nuclear plants are extremely expensive to build and decommission.

> **In my opinion**, nuclear power can be part of the **energy mix**, but it should not be the main solution. Investing in solar, wind, and energy storage is safer and more sustainable in the long run.
